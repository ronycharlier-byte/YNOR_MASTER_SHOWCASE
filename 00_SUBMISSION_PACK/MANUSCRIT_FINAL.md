# A Minimal Structural Ontology for Quantum Reality

## Abstract

The quantum real is not adequately captured by a framework of substances endowed with intrinsic properties, nor by entities embedded in a pre-existing spacetime. It is more appropriately described as a structure of constraints and contextual correlations organizing physical possibilities, some of which become locally determined through event-based actualizations. These actualizations, linked by the propagation of structural constraints, induce a relational ordering identifiable as time and a configuration of coexistence defining space. Objects, identity, and information emerge as stabilized features of this underlying ontological organization.

To articulate this framework, we introduce a methodological principle, denoted by \(\mu\), which selects ontologically admissible components of the quantum formalism by maximizing explanatory power while minimizing ontological cost and interpretative friction. The resulting structure provides a minimal, coherent, and interpretation-neutral ontology compatible with standard quantum mechanics.

## Keywords

Quantum mechanics, ontology, structural realism, contextuality, emergence, spacetime, information

## 1. Introduction

The interpretation of quantum mechanics continues to raise fundamental questions concerning the nature of physical reality. Standard ontological frameworks typically assume that reality consists of substances endowed with intrinsic properties evolving within a pre-existing spacetime. However, key features of quantum theory, most notably contextuality, non-classical correlations, and the absence of globally defined properties, challenge the adequacy of such assumptions.

These challenges are not merely interpretive. They indicate that the formal structure of quantum theory resists a straightforward translation into a classical ontology of local objects with intrinsic attributes. The theory does not simply describe what is already there; it constrains what can be meaningfully said to be there under specific experimental arrangements.

In response, this paper proposes an alternative ontological framework in which the quantum real is not conceived as a collection of entities, but as a structure of constraints and contextual correlations organizing physical possibilities. Within this perspective, physical determination occurs through localized event-based actualizations, while familiar notions such as objects, identity, space, and time are reconstructed as emergent features of an underlying relational organization.

The aim is not to advance a specific interpretation of quantum mechanics, but to articulate a minimal and coherent ontological framework compatible with its formal structure. The approach is deliberately interpretation-neutral: it seeks to identify the smallest set of ontological commitments required to preserve the structure of the theory without reintroducing classical primitives.

## 2. Ontological Framework

### 2.1 Structural Primacy

The quantum real is described as a structure of constraints and contextual correlations organizing physical possibilities, without presupposing substances with intrinsic properties or a pre-existing spacetime. Structure is therefore ontologically prior to objecthood.

### 2.2 Contextuality

No property is fully determinate independently of a context of actualization. Properties are not treated as pre-existing contents simply revealed by measurement, but as determinations that arise relative to a measurement or interaction context.

### 2.3 Non-Factorizability

The global structure may be non-factorisable, so that the possibilities available to a composite system do not reduce to independent local structures. The ontological significance of entanglement lies precisely in this non-additivity: the whole constrains the parts in a way that cannot be reconstructed from local properties alone.

### 2.4 Event-Based Actualization

Within this structure, certain possibilities become locally determined through event-based actualizations. These events constitute the fundamental instances at which physical determination occurs.

### 2.5 Structural Causality

Causality is not conceived as the transmission of properties between entities, but as the propagation of structural constraints organizing transitions between events. Causal order is thus derivative of the organization of the structure rather than a primitive feature of substances.

### 2.6 Emergent Time

Time corresponds to the relational ordering of events induced by structural constraints and does not exist independently of them. Temporal order is therefore an emergent feature of the sequence of actualizations.

### 2.7 Emergent Space

Space is defined by the relational configuration of coexistence, separation, and coupling between events and stabilized nodes, rather than as a pre-existing container. Spatial structure is reconstructed from relational patterns rather than assumed as a fundamental background.

### 2.8 Decoherence and Stabilization

Stable structures emerge when interaction dynamics select, via decoherence, robust correlations, privileged bases, and persistent descriptions. Decoherence does not create reality; it selects stable modes of local description within the global structure.

### 2.9 Objects and Identity

Objects correspond to stabilized nodes within the structure, and identity to the continuity of such stabilization across compatible events. Neither notion is ontologically primitive.

### 2.10 Information as Structural Form

Information is identified with the structural form of the constraints linking possibilities, correlations, and actualizations. It is neither merely subjective nor purely abstract, but structurally encoded in the organization of admissible outcomes.

### 2.11 Subsystems

A subsystem is a contextual restriction of the global structure, defined relative to a domain of accessible interactions. Subsystems are real, but derivative: they are not ontologically autonomous wholes.

### 2.12 Ontological Synthesis

The physical real is thus best understood as a relational organization from which events, causality, time, space, objects, identity, and information emerge as derived features of a common structural basis.

## 3. Relation to the Quantum Formalism

The proposed framework aligns naturally with the standard formalism of quantum mechanics.

A state does not encode a list of intrinsic properties, but a structure of possibilities constrained by context. The Born rule assigns probabilities to outcomes relative to a measurement arrangement. Non-commutativity expresses structural incompatibility among observables. Entanglement captures the non-factorisable character of composite systems. Decoherence explains the emergence of stable local descriptions without requiring a classical substratum.

In this sense, the ontology does not reinterpret the formalism from outside. Rather, it reads the formalism as already expressing a structure of constrained possibilities and contextual actualizations.

## 4. Formal Sketch

This section provides a minimal formal articulation of the proposed ontological framework within the standard formalism of quantum mechanics. The aim is not to construct a complete theory, but to identify the mathematical structures corresponding to the core ontological commitments.

### 4.1 Hilbert Structure and Global State

Let \(\mathcal H\) be the Hilbert space associated with the total system, and let \(\rho\) be a density operator on \(\mathcal H\). In the present framework, \(\rho\) does not represent a collection of intrinsic properties, but encodes a structure of probabilistic constraints on possible outcomes. For any measurement context, it determines a family of probability distributions via the Born rule.

Composite systems are represented by tensor products,
\[
\mathcal H_{AB} = \mathcal H_A \otimes \mathcal H_B,
\]
and, in general, the global state is non-factorisable,
\[
\rho_{AB} \neq \rho_A \otimes \rho_B.
\]
This non-factorisability encodes the structural character of quantum correlations.

### 4.2 Contexts and Observables

Let \(\mathcal C\) denote a set of measurement or interaction contexts. Each context \(c \in \mathcal C\) is associated with a POVM \(\{\Pi^c_e\}\), where \(e\) labels possible outcomes.

The probability of an event \(e\) in context \(c\) is given by
\[
p(e \mid c) = \mathrm{Tr}(\rho \,\Pi^c_e).
\]

Contexts therefore define admissible decompositions of the structure of possibilities, rather than revealing pre-existing properties. The dependence on \(c\) expresses the contextual character of physical determination.

### 4.3 Events and Actualization

An event corresponds to the realization of an outcome \(e\) within a context \(c\). Formally, actualization may be represented as a transition
\[
(\rho, c) \longrightarrow e,
\]
followed by an update of the state,
\[
\rho \longrightarrow \rho'.
\]

This update is not interpreted as the disclosure of a pre-existing value, but as the inscription of a determination within the structure of correlations. The framework remains neutral with respect to the precise mechanism of this transition.

### 4.4 Decoherence and Structural Selection

Let \(\mathcal D\) be a completely positive, trace-preserving map describing the effective dynamics of a system interacting with an environment. In typical situations, one may write
\[
\mathcal D(\rho_{SE}) = \mathrm{Tr}_E(\rho_{SE}),
\]
or, more generally, use a reduced dynamics that suppresses interference terms in a preferred basis.

Decoherence does not eliminate superpositions at the global level, but selects locally stable structures by:

- suppressing interference between certain components,
- privileging specific bases,
- stabilizing correlations that persist under environmental coupling.

It therefore functions as a mechanism of structural selection rather than ontological reduction.

### 4.5 Emergent Causal, Temporal, and Spatial Structures

Let \(\mathcal E\) denote the set of actualized events. A causal relation is introduced as a partial order
\[
e_i \preceq e_j,
\]
indicating that the occurrence of \(e_i\) constrains the admissible possibilities for \(e_j\).

The pair \((\mathcal E, \preceq)\) defines an emergent temporal structure. In addition, a relation of coupling or proximity,
\[
\sim \subseteq \mathcal E \times \mathcal E,
\]
defines a graph
\[
\mathcal G = (\mathcal E, \sim).
\]

This graph encodes the relational structure underlying spatial organization. Spatial notions are thus derived from patterns of interaction and correlation rather than assumed a priori.

### 4.6 Summary

The formal structure can be summarized as
\[
\mathfrak Q = (\mathcal H,\ \mathcal C,\ \rho,\ \{\Pi^c_e\},\ \mathcal D,\ \preceq,\ \sim).
\]

Within this framework:

- \(\rho\) encodes constraints on possibilities,
- \(\mathcal C\) and \(\{\Pi^c_e\}\) define contexts of actualization,
- events correspond to realized outcomes,
- decoherence selects stable structures,
- temporal and spatial relations emerge from constraints on events.

## 5. Methods: Ontological Selection via the \(\mu\)-Principle

### 5.1 Methodological Principle (\(\mu\))

The formalism of quantum mechanics is well established, yet its ontological interpretation remains underdetermined. Multiple interpretative frameworks reproduce the same empirical predictions while introducing different degrees of conceptual complexity, ontological commitment, and interpretative ambiguity.

To address this underdetermination, we introduce a methodological principle, denoted by \(\mu\), which functions as a criterion of ontological selection. The purpose of this principle is not to modify the formalism of quantum mechanics, but to evaluate which of its structural components should be regarded as fundamental within a minimal and coherent ontological framework.

Each component \(B_i\) of the formalism is assigned a score:
\[
\mu(B_i)=w_\alpha \alpha_i - w_\beta \beta_i - w_\kappa \kappa_i,
\]
where \(\alpha_i\) measures explanatory power and empirical adequacy, \(\beta_i\) measures ontological and formal cost, and \(\kappa_i\) measures interpretative friction. The weights \(w_\alpha, w_\beta, w_\kappa > 0\) define the methodological balance between these criteria.

The ontological status of each component is determined by the value of \(\mu(B_i)\):

- \(\mu \gg 0\): fundamental
- \(\mu > 0\): retained
- \(\mu \approx 0\): retained but interpretation-neutralized
- \(\mu < 0\): reduced or treated as effective
- \(\mu \ll 0\): excluded at the ontological level

At the global level, the admissible structure is defined by
\[
\mathfrak Q_\mu = \arg\max_{\mathfrak Q}\sum_i \mu(B_i).
\]

In this sense, the \(\mu\)-principle functions as a form of ontological regularization, selecting structures that maximize explanatory coherence while minimizing ontological overhead and interpretative ambiguity.

### 5.2 Quantitative \(\mu\)-Assessment (0–5 Scale)

To make the \(\mu\)-principle operational, we introduce a normalized scoring scheme on the interval \([0,5]\), with baseline symmetric weighting \(w_\alpha = w_\beta = w_\kappa = 1\). Each component is evaluated according to
\[
\mu(B_i)=\alpha_i-\beta_i-\kappa_i.
\]

This yields a comparative index of ontological economy.

#### Core Quantum Blocks

| Component | \(\alpha\) | \(\beta\) | \(\kappa\) | \(\mu\) | Status |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|
| Hilbert space \(\mathcal H\) | 5 | 1 | 1 | +3 | Fundamental |
| State \(\rho\) | 5 | 1 | 1 | +3 | Fundamental |
| Born rule | 5 | 0 | 0 | +5 | Indispensable |
| POVM / contexts | 4 | 1 | 1 | +2 | Retained |
| Non-commutativity | 4 | 0 | 1 | +3 | Fundamental |
| Entanglement | 5 | 1 | 1–2 | +3 to +2 | Fundamental |
| Partial trace | 4 | 1 | 1 | +2 | Retained |
| Decoherence | 4 | 1 | 1 | +2 | Structural selection |
| Minimal actualization | 4 | 1 | 2 | +1 | Neutralized |

#### Rejected or Low-\(\mu\) Structures

| Component | \(\alpha\) | \(\beta\) | \(\kappa\) | \(\mu\) | Status |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|
| Classical local realism | 1 | 4 | 5 | -8 | Excluded |
| Local hidden variables | 2 | 4 | 4 | -6 | Excluded |
| Intrinsic property ontology | 1 | 4 | 4 | -7 | Excluded |
| Ad hoc collapse (unformalized) | 3 | 3 | 4 | -4 | Not retained |

### 5.3 Regime Interpretation

The normalized scores define distinct regimes:

- \(\mu \ge 3\): structural core, interpretation-independent
- \(1 \le \mu < 3\): retained, structurally interpreted
- \(\mu \approx 1\): necessary but unresolved
- \(\mu < 0\): excluded as ontological primitives

The resulting high-\(\mu\) core corresponds to the Hilbert-space structure, the quantum state, the Born rule, and non-commutativity. Medium-\(\mu\) components, such as POVMs, decoherence, and partial trace, are retained as operationally indispensable but ontologically derivative.

### 5.4 Selection Rule

The admissible quantum structure is therefore
\[
\mathfrak Q_\mu = \{ B_i \mid \mu(B_i) > 0 \}.
\]

This criterion selects the standard quantum formalism stripped of classical ontological surcharge, preserving its predictive and structural content while minimizing unnecessary commitments.

### 5.5 Robustness

The ranking is stable under moderate variations of the weights. Increasing \(w_\alpha\) reinforces the quantum structural core, while increasing \(w_\kappa\) penalizes ad hoc interpretative additions. The resulting ordering is therefore methodologically robust, even though the precise numerical assignments remain heuristic.

### 5.6 Transition to Formal Structure

The \(\mu\)-principle identifies the subset of quantum structures that should be retained in a minimal ontology. The next step is to articulate these selected components within a coherent formal framework.

## 6. Discussion

### 6.1 Ontological Economy

The proposed framework achieves a high degree of ontological economy by avoiding the postulation of substances with intrinsic properties or a pre-existing spacetime background. All core notions are derived from a single structural basis, thereby reducing ontological redundancy while preserving conceptual coherence.

This economy reflects a principled commitment to limiting ontological assumptions to what is required by the formal structure of quantum theory. The framework therefore aims to remain explanatorily sufficient without introducing unnecessary metaphysical commitments.

### 6.2 Compatibility with Quantum Formalism

The framework aligns with key features of quantum mechanics. Contextuality is captured through structural dependence, non-classical correlations are incorporated at the level of constraints, and the absence of globally defined properties is reflected in the event-based nature of actualization.

Entanglement, in particular, strongly supports the structural reading. It shows that the physically relevant information of composite systems is often carried by the global state rather than by local properties taken separately. The formalism thus naturally supports a non-additive, relational ontology.

Importantly, the framework does not introduce hidden variables, classical substrates, or additional ontological layers beyond what is minimally required. It therefore remains consistent with the constraints imposed by the formalism.

### 6.3 Relation to Interpretations

The proposed framework is compatible with a range of interpretative approaches, including relational formulations and certain forms of structural realism, while remaining open to non-classical realist extensions.

It should therefore be understood not as a competing interpretation, but as a meta-ontological reconstruction aimed at identifying a stable conceptual core across interpretative variations. It does not resolve the measurement problem in a way that would privilege one interpretation over another; instead, it clarifies the ontological space within which such interpretations can be compared.

### 6.4 Status of Events

The ontological status of event actualization remains the least settled part of the framework. The proposal intentionally remains neutral with respect to whether one prefers collapse-based, Everettian, relational, or other accounts of actualization.

What the framework insists on is that determination is real, local, and structurally constrained. An event is not reduced to a subjective update of knowledge, nor to the revelation of a pre-existing classical property. It is the point at which a possibility becomes determined within the network of correlations.

### 6.5 Emergence of Classicality

Within this framework, objects, identity, and spacetime are not fundamental but emergent. Their apparent stability arises from repeated structural constraints, coherence across sequences of events, and the stabilization of relational patterns.

This provides a conceptual basis for the emergence of classical descriptions without presupposing classical primitives, thereby offering a coherent account of the quantum-to-classical transition. The classical appears because some structural patterns become reliable enough to support effective descriptions in terms of persistent objects, trajectories, and local interactions.

### 6.6 Limits and Open Questions

Several issues remain to be clarified. The formal representation of structural constraints is still schematic, and the integration of this framework with quantum field theory and quantum gravity remains open. Likewise, the mathematical realization of emergent spacetime demands further development.

These limitations do not undermine the framework, but rather indicate avenues for its extension. The present proposal is intentionally minimal and foundational, and its value lies in clarifying the conceptual architecture within which more detailed theories may later be formulated.

### 6.7 Conceptual Outcome

The framework supports the following thesis:

> The physical real is not fundamentally composed of entities, but of structured constraints whose organization gives rise to all observable features of reality.

This thesis preserves realism without substantialism, and structure without abstraction. It treats the quantum world as ontologically serious while rejecting the assumption that seriousness requires classical objects with intrinsic properties.

### 6.8 Positioning within Structural Realism

The framework is best situated within structural realism, but with an important qualification. It is stronger than an instrumentalist reading because it attributes ontological weight to the structure itself. At the same time, it remains more cautious than a fully radical version of structural realism, insofar as it does not claim that all questions about underlying ontological support are definitively settled.

Its position may therefore be described as a structurally committed, interpretation-neutral realism: the structure is real, the objects are derivative, and the classical world is emergent.

### 6.9 Comparison with Existing Approaches

To situate the present proposal within the landscape of quantum foundations, it is useful to compare it with several influential approaches. While it shares important commitments with some of them, it is not reducible to any single interpretation.

#### 6.9.1 Everettian Approaches

The framework shares with Everettian approaches a commitment to the standard quantum formalism and to the rejection of fundamental collapse as a necessary component of the theory. It also agrees that the formalism should be taken seriously at the ontological level rather than treated merely as an epistemic tool.

However, the present proposal differs in its ontological restraint. It does not identify the global quantum state with a branching plurality of worlds, nor does it require full metaphysical commitment to ontic branching. Instead, it treats the quantum state as encoding a structure of constraints and contextual possibilities. In this sense, it is compatible with unitary evolution, but it remains neutral with respect to the metaphysics of branching.

#### 6.9.2 Bohmian Approaches

Bohmian mechanics offers a robust realist account by supplementing the quantum formalism with particle positions guided by a pilot wave. Its chief strength lies in its clear ontology and deterministic dynamics.

The present framework shares with Bohmian approaches the conviction that quantum theory should support a realist reading, and it also recognizes the significance of non-local structure. Yet it departs from Bohmian ontology by not postulating hidden trajectories or an underlying particle ontology with additional dynamical variables. It seeks instead to recover realism at the level of structure, correlations, and event-based actualization, without introducing a separate hidden substrate.

#### 6.9.3 QBist Approaches

QBism emphasizes the role of the agent and interprets the quantum state as a tool for encoding personal degrees of belief about future experiences. This approach is valuable insofar as it foregrounds the contextual and decision-relative aspects of quantum theory.

The present framework, however, is not subjectivist. It does not interpret the quantum state as a private bookkeeping device for an agent’s expectations, but as an objective structure of constraints on admissible possibilities. While it agrees that context is indispensable, it rejects the reduction of the formalism to individual belief. The ontology proposed here is relational, but not epistemically private.

#### 6.9.4 Relational Quantum Mechanics

Relational quantum mechanics is perhaps the closest conceptual neighbour of the present proposal. Both frameworks reject the idea of observer-independent properties defined in a classical sense, and both treat physical determination as context-dependent.

The present framework shares this relational orientation, but it places stronger emphasis on structural primacy and ontological economy. In particular, it does not merely claim that properties are relative to interactions; it argues that the quantum real is fundamentally a structure of constraints and contextual correlations from which objects, identities, and classical descriptions emerge. The framework is therefore relational, but explicitly structural in its ontological commitments.

#### 6.9.5 Summary of the Comparative Position

The framework proposed in this paper can therefore be characterized as follows:

- it is more realist than QBism,
- less ontologically committed than Bohmian mechanics,
- less metaphysically expansive than Everettian branching,
- and more structurally explicit than relational interpretations.

Its distinctive contribution lies in identifying a minimal structural core that preserves realism while avoiding both ontological inflation and epistemic reduction. Rather than choosing between competing metaphysical pictures, it isolates the set of commitments that remain stable across interpretations and directly supported by the formal structure of quantum theory.

> In this respect, the framework may be described as a structurally committed, interpretation-neutral realism: the structure is real, objects are derivative, and classicality emerges from stabilized patterns within the relational organization of constraints.

## 7. Conclusion

The framework developed in this paper provides a minimal structural ontology for quantum reality. It replaces substances with constraints, intrinsic properties with contextual actualizations, and spacetime with emergent relational orders. In doing so, it offers a coherent and parsimonious account of the quantum real as an ontologically structured yet non-classical domain.

Rather than resolving all interpretative disputes, this approach establishes a minimal conceptual foundation upon which such disputes can be meaningfully evaluated. Its central claim is that the quantum real is best understood not as a collection of things, but as an organization of relations from which the familiar categories of physics emerge.

The methodological principle \(\mu\) plays a central role in this reconstruction. It selects the structures of the quantum formalism that maximize explanatory power and structural coherence while minimizing ontological cost and interpretative friction. In doing so, it provides a principled criterion for distinguishing fundamental from derivative components within the formalism.

Taken together, the ontological framework, the formal sketch, and the \(\mu\)-criterion yield a unified proposal: quantum mechanics is most economically understood as the expression of a relational structure whose stable patterns generate the appearance of objects, time, space, and classicality, without requiring a classical substratum beneath them.
